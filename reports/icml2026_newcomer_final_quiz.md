# ICML 2026 Newcomer Final Quiz

This quiz checks whether you can understand and use the conference map. It is open-book. You may use the roadmap, glossary, area tour, trend lessons, paper course, figures, and source reports.

Do not open the answer key until you finish your first attempt.

## Instructions

- Total points: 100
- Suggested time: 90 to 120 minutes
- Passing score: 80
- You must earn at least half the points in every part.
- A serious interpretation error prevents a passing result until corrected.

Serious interpretation errors include:

- treating AlphaXiv attention as a direct research-quality score;
- treating oral or award selection as proof that every paper in an area is better;
- treating a visible GitHub link as proof of reproducibility;
- presenting a directional pattern as a settled causal fact;
- hiding a major known limitation when making a strong claim.

## Part A: Key Ideas and Terms — 15 Points

Answer each question in two or three sentences. Each question is worth 3 points.

### A1. Taxonomy

What is a taxonomy, and why should the 12-area map be treated as a navigation tool rather than final truth?

### A2. Enrichment

An area has oral enrichment of 1.4. Explain what this means. Explain one thing it does not mean.

### A3. Public Attention

What does AlphaXiv attention measure in this project? Name two reasons it may differ from scientific importance.

### A4. Historical Baseline

Why does comparing ICML 2026 with nearby conference paper collections improve the analysis? Name one limitation of the comparison.

### A5. Artifact Visibility

Explain the difference between a visible artifact link and a reproducible result.

## Part C: Evidence and Interpretation — 25 Points

Each case is worth 5 points. For every case, write:

1. what the evidence supports;
2. what it does not support;
3. one useful next check.

### C1. Robotics Attention

Robotics, Embodiment, and World Models contains 195 papers, or 2.9% of the mapped corpus. Its public-attention enrichment is 2.11.

A colleague says: “Robotics is the most important area at ICML 2026.”

Evaluate the claim.

### C2. Theory Signals

Theory, Optimization, and Algorithms has oral enrichment of 1.45 and public-attention enrichment of 0.46.

A colleague says: “The community is losing interest in theory, so theory did not matter to the conference.”

Evaluate the claim.

### C3. Multimodal Baseline

Multimodal, Vision, and Perception contains 889 papers, or 13.4% of the mapped corpus. Its share is 3.1 percentage points below the nearby venue baseline used by the project.

Explain how the area can be both large and underweight.

### C4. Repository Link

A paper has a GitHub URL in the AlphaXiv metadata and the repository has many stars.

A colleague writes: “The result has been reproduced by the community.”

Evaluate the claim.

### C5. The Largest Area

The LLM reasoning and post-training area contains 1,099 papers, but several subarea clusters still need review.

Write one safe headline and one unsafe headline about this evidence. Explain the difference.

## Part D: New Paper Cases — 20 Points

These cases are fictional. You have not seen them in the course. This part tests whether you can transfer what you learned.

Each case is worth 10 points:

- likely area and possible neighboring area: 2 points;
- main contribution: 2 points;
- evidence assessment: 2 points;
- important limit: 2 points;
- connection to a conference trend: 2 points.

### D1. Tool-Using Coding Agent

A paper introduces an agent that edits a software repository, runs tests, and asks for help when it is uncertain. The paper reports a 12-point improvement on one coding benchmark. The new system uses the same base model as the comparison system but adds a planning module, a memory store, and three new tools. The authors release a project page, but the benchmark scripts and agent code are not yet public.

Answer:

1. What is the most likely area? What neighboring area may also matter?
2. Is the main contribution more likely to be a new learned capability, a better system, or a new theory? Why?
3. What does the reported improvement support?
4. What important questions remain?
5. Which ICML 2026 trend does the case illustrate?

### D2. Protein Design Model

A paper introduces a model that generates protein structures in several stages, from a rough shape to atomic detail. It reports better structure-validity scores than three generative baselines on a public benchmark. It does not test whether the generated proteins work in a laboratory. The training data overlap with the benchmark is not discussed.

Answer:

1. What is the most likely area? What method area may overlap?
2. What is the main contribution?
3. What does the evaluation support?
4. What does the evaluation fail to establish?
5. Which two additional checks would most improve confidence?

## Part E: Your ICML 2026 Briefing — 20 Points

Write 350 to 500 words for a technically curious newcomer.

Your briefing must include:

1. a one-sentence description of the conference map;
2. three major trends;
3. one contrast between program signal and public attention;
4. at least two representative papers;
5. two important caveats;
6. one open question or next research direction.

Use at least three pieces of numerical evidence. You may round numbers. Name the source type when it matters, such as “AlphaXiv attention” or “oral enrichment.”

## Final Self-Check

Before you finish, ask:

- Did I separate area-level patterns from paper-level judgments?
