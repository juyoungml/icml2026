# Newcomer Learning Roadmap: Goal and Design Brief

## The Main Goal

Create a friendly, step-by-step learning journey that helps a newcomer understand the main ideas and research directions represented at ICML 2026.

By the end, the learner should be able to explain the broad shape of the conference, describe the most important trends, compare different kinds of evidence, discuss a small set of representative papers, and clearly state what is still uncertain.

The learner does not need to read most of the 6,628 papers. The aim is broad understanding, not complete coverage.

## What “Understand About 80%” Means

The phrase “understand 80%” should describe useful understanding rather than a literal percentage of papers.

A learner has reached the goal when they can:

1. Describe the 12 broad research areas in plain language.
2. Explain the five or six patterns that shape the main ICML 2026 story.
3. Distinguish paper volume, oral and award selection, public attention, historical comparison, and artifact visibility.
4. Give representative examples without treating one paper as proof of a whole trend.
5. Explain the most important caveats in the data and taxonomy.
6. Read a new ICML paper and place it in the larger landscape.
7. Give a short, balanced briefing about ICML 2026 without overstating the evidence.

This is closer to “understand most of the map and know how to explore the rest” than “know most of the technical details.”

## Intended Learner

The main learner is someone who:

- knows basic ideas such as training, evaluation, models, and datasets;
- may not know the current language used in every ML subfield;
- has not followed thousands of ICML papers;
- wants a guided route instead of a large list of links;
- is willing to read a small number of carefully selected papers or summaries.

Every technical term should be explained the first time it appears. The writing should use normal words, short sentences, and concrete examples.

## The Learning Promise

If a learner follows the standard roadmap, completes the short activities, and passes the final assessment, they should be able to:

- talk comfortably about the main ICML 2026 research directions;
- explain why different signals tell different stories;
- recognize weak or overstated trend claims;
- choose which area or papers to study next;
- teach the main conference story to another newcomer.

## What This Roadmap Will Not Promise

The roadmap will not make someone an expert in every technical area. It will not replace reading full papers, reproducing experiments, or learning the mathematical foundations of each field.

It also will not present the current taxonomy as final truth. Many project-level conclusions are still waiting for manual paper review. The roadmap must clearly separate checked facts, directional patterns, and open questions.

## Proposed Learning Journey

The roadmap should follow a repeated pattern:

> Learn the idea → see an example → compare the evidence → answer a check question → explain it in your own words.

### Step 0: Learn How to Use the Roadmap

Goal: understand what the journey covers and choose a pace.

The learner chooses one of three routes:

- Quick tour: about 3 to 4 hours, focused on the conference map and major patterns.
- Standard route: about 10 to 12 hours, including representative papers and exercises.
- Deep route: about 20 hours, including extra papers, comparisons, and a final written briefing.

### Step 1: Learn How the Conference Map Was Built

Goal: understand the source data and why the map is useful but imperfect.

Topics:

- the 6,628-paper conference corpus;
- the 12 broad areas;
- the difference between a topic label and a paper’s full contribution;
- why some papers sit between areas;
- why manual review matters.

Checkpoint: explain why a taxonomy is a navigation aid rather than unquestionable truth.

### Step 2: Tour the 12 Research Areas

Goal: build a mental map of the conference.

For each area, provide:

- a one-sentence description;
- two or three common research questions;
- one representative paper;
- one connection to another area;
- one common misunderstanding.

Checkpoint: place a small set of sample papers into likely areas and explain any uncertainty.

### Step 3: Understand the Main Conference Story

Goal: learn the strongest broad patterns.

Core patterns:

1. LLM reasoning and post-training form the largest current area in this taxonomy.
2. Systems, efficiency, agents, and code-related work have strong presence relative to nearby venue baselines.
3. Theory and safety receive stronger program signal than public attention alone suggests.
4. Robotics and world-model work receive high public attention despite a smaller paper share.
5. Multimodal and vision work is large inside ICML but looks smaller relative to nearby venue baselines.
6. Artifact links are visible in several fast-moving areas, but a link does not prove reproducibility.

Checkpoint: explain each pattern, name the evidence behind it, and give one reason to be cautious.

### Step 4: Learn to Read Different Signals

Goal: avoid treating every number as a measure of quality.

Signals to compare:

- number and share of papers;
- oral and award selection;
- AlphaXiv votes and visits;
- comparison with nearby conferences;
- broad arXiv growth;
- GitHub and artifact links.

Checkpoint: match each signal to what it can support and what it cannot support.

### Step 5: Read Representative Papers

Goal: connect the conference-level map to real research.

The standard route should use a small core set of about 12 papers. The set should include:

- at least one paper from each major area group;
- both high-public and high-program examples;
- at least two papers that are hard to classify;
- at least one theory paper;
- at least one safety paper;
- at least one systems or agents paper;
- at least one robotics or multimodal paper;
- at least one paper with an artifact that can be checked.

Each paper lesson should answer:

1. What problem does the paper address?
2. What does it add?
3. What evidence does it provide?
4. What are its limits?
5. Why does it matter for the conference map?

Checkpoint: complete a short paper card in the learner’s own words.

### Step 6: Compare Areas and Find Tensions

Goal: move beyond a list of topics and understand the relationships between them.

Examples:

- reasoning performance versus reasoning reliability;
- larger models versus efficient training and inference;
- agent capability versus tool safety;
- public excitement versus committee selection;
- benchmark gains versus real-world usefulness;
- visible code links versus tested reproducibility.

Checkpoint: choose one tension and explain both sides using at least two sources.

### Step 7: Build a Personal View of ICML 2026

Goal: synthesize the material rather than repeat the project’s wording.

Final activity:

- prepare a five-minute spoken briefing or a one-page written briefing;
- describe the main conference map;
- explain three major trends;
- discuss one surprising contrast;
- include two important caveats;
- recommend three papers for further study.

## Quiz and Assessment Design

The assessment should test explanation and judgment, not only memory. It should be open-book because real research work depends on finding, comparing, and using evidence correctly.

### Part A: Key Ideas and Terms — 15 points

Short questions about terms such as taxonomy, program signal, public attention, historical baseline, and artifact visibility.

Purpose: check that the learner can understand the rest of the project without getting lost in vocabulary.

### Part B: The Conference Map — 20 points

Questions about the 12 areas, their relationships, and representative research questions.

Purpose: check broad coverage without requiring exact memorization of every count.

### Part C: Evidence and Interpretation — 25 points

Small tables, chart excerpts, or short claims that the learner must interpret.

Example task: “Robotics has a small paper share and high public attention. What can we reasonably conclude, and what can we not conclude?”

Purpose: check whether the learner understands what each signal means.

### Part D: Paper Cases — 20 points

Two short paper summaries. The learner identifies the likely area, contribution, evidence, limitations, and relevance to a trend.

Purpose: check whether the learner can transfer the map to papers they have not memorized.

### Part E: Synthesis — 20 points

One short briefing or essay that explains the conference story with evidence and caveats.

Purpose: check whether the learner can combine ideas into a balanced view.

## Passing Standard

A learner demonstrates broad understanding when they meet all three conditions:

- score at least 80 out of 100 overall;
- score at least half of the available points in every section;
- avoid any serious interpretation error, such as calling public attention a quality score or calling a GitHub link proof of reproducibility.

Suggested result levels:

- 90–100: Can explain and teach the landscape with good judgment.
- 80–89: Has strong broad understanding and can explore independently.
- 65–79: Understands the map but should revisit weak areas.
- Below 65: Should repeat the core lessons before taking the final assessment again.

## Feedback and Remediation

Every quiz question should include:

- the correct or best answer;
- a short explanation;
- why tempting wrong answers are wrong;
- a link to the lesson that teaches the idea;
- a follow-up question when the learner needs more practice.

The score report should group mistakes into useful categories:

- conference map;
- technical vocabulary;
- evidence interpretation;
- paper reading;
- uncertainty and caveats;
- synthesis and communication.

This lets the learner repeat only the parts they have not yet understood.

## Quality Rules for the Tutorial

The final tutorial should:

- use friendly and direct language;
- explain new terms immediately;
- keep each lesson focused on one main idea;
- use examples before adding detail;
- state expected time and learning goals at the start of each lesson;
- end every lesson with a recap and a small activity;
- distinguish facts, directional evidence, and open questions;
- offer optional depth without interrupting the main path;
- keep the standard reading list small enough to finish;
- include working links to reports, figures, paper pages, and answer explanations.

## Proposed Project Deliverables

1. A main newcomer roadmap with quick, standard, and deep routes.
2. A plain-language glossary.
3. Twelve short area guides.
4. Six trend lessons built around the major conference patterns.
5. About 12 representative paper lessons.
6. Small checkpoint questions after every lesson.
7. A comprehensive final quiz.
8. An answer key with explanations and source links.
9. A scoring guide and personalized review paths.
10. A final briefing template that learners can complete.

## Success Criteria for the Project

The roadmap is ready when:

- a newcomer can follow it without needing another navigation document;
- the standard route can be completed in about 10 to 12 hours;
- every major claim links to evidence and a caveat;
- the core paper set covers the major patterns and important counterexamples;
- the quiz tests all stated learning outcomes;
- the answer key explains reasoning rather than only listing answers;
- all links and generated data checks pass;
- at least one test reader can complete the route and produce a balanced final briefing.
