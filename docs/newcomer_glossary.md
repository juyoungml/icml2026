# Plain-Language ICML 2026 Glossary

This glossary explains the words used in the newcomer course. Definitions are intentionally short. They describe how the words are used in this project.

## A

**Agent:** A model-based system that chooses steps, uses tools, or interacts with an environment to complete a task.

**AlphaXiv attention:** Votes or visits recorded by AlphaXiv. It can show public interest on that platform. It is not a score of research quality.

**Artifact:** Something released with a paper, such as code, data, model weights, a benchmark, or a project page.

**Artifact visibility:** Whether the project found a visible artifact link. Visibility does not mean that the artifact works or reproduces the result.

## B

**Baseline:** A method or result used for comparison. A new method should normally be compared with relevant baselines.

**Benchmark:** A shared task, dataset, and measurement setup used to compare methods.

## C

**Causal learning:** Work that tries to understand cause and effect, not only statistical association.

**Caveat:** A reason to limit or qualify a statement.

**Checked fact:** A statement directly supported by official metadata or a simple count in this workspace.

**Cluster:** A group of papers found through similarities in text or semantic representation. A cluster is a tool for exploration, not a perfect scientific category.

## D

**Dataset:** A collection of examples used for training, testing, or analysis.

**Directional pattern:** A pattern supported well enough to guide reading, but not yet checked enough for strong publication language.

**Distribution shift:** A change between the data used to develop a model and the data seen later.

## E

**Enrichment:** How common a signal is in an area compared with the conference average. An enrichment of 1.0 means average. Above 1.0 means more common than average. Below 1.0 means less common.

**Evaluation:** The process used to test a method and measure its behavior.

**Evidence:** Information used to support or weaken a claim. Evidence may include experiments, proofs, comparisons, ablations, or observed counts.

## F

**Federated learning:** Training across separate clients or devices without simply collecting all raw data in one place.

**Foundation model:** A model trained broadly enough to be adapted to many later tasks.

## G

**Generalization:** A model’s ability to work on examples or situations it did not simply memorize during training.

**Generative model:** A model that creates content such as text, images, video, audio, or structured data.

## H

**Historical baseline:** A comparison with accepted-paper collections from nearby conferences or years. It gives context, but differences in venue scope and metadata can affect the result.

## I

**Inference:** Using a trained model to produce a prediction, decision, or generated output.

## L

**Large language model, or LLM:** A language model trained on a large amount of data and usually built with many parameters.

## M

**Manual review:** A person checks a paper, classification, artifact, or claim instead of relying only on automated labels.

**Metric:** A number used to measure performance or behavior.

**Multimodal model:** A model that works with more than one kind of information, such as text and images.

## O

**Open question:** A question that the current evidence does not settle.

**Oral enrichment:** How often papers from an area receive oral selection compared with the conference average. It is an area-level program signal, not a quality score for every paper.

## P

**Paper share:** The percentage of mapped conference papers assigned to an area.

**Post-training:** Training or adaptation performed after broad pretraining. Examples include instruction tuning, preference learning, and reinforcement learning.

**Program signal:** Information from conference decisions such as oral or award selection. It shows selection emphasis, not a complete ranking of scientific value.

**Public attention:** Interest visible through a public platform. It may favor accessible topics, demos, famous teams, or strong project pages.

## R

**Reinforcement learning, or RL:** Learning through actions and feedback, often expressed as rewards.

**RL with verifiable rewards, or RLVR:** Reinforcement learning where an answer can be checked automatically, as in some mathematics or coding tasks.

**Reproducibility:** The ability to repeat a method or experiment closely enough to check its result. A repository link alone does not establish reproducibility.

## S

**Semantic cluster:** A group formed using similarity in meaning rather than only exact shared words.

**Signal:** A piece of evidence that reveals one part of the landscape. Different signals answer different questions.

**Subarea:** A narrower research topic inside one of the 12 broad areas.

**Systems research:** Work on the computing methods and infrastructure used to train, run, store, or serve models.

## T

**Taxonomy:** A named grouping system used to organize papers. The project taxonomy helps people navigate the conference but is still being checked.

**Test-time compute:** Extra computation used while a model is answering, such as search, repeated sampling, or verification.

**Trend:** A repeated pattern across papers or signals. A trend is not automatically a cause and does not describe every paper.

## V

**Venue baseline:** A comparison with the paper mix at other research conferences.

**Vision-language-action model, or VLA:** A model that connects visual input, language instructions, and actions, often for robotics.

## W

**World model:** A learned representation that tries to predict how an environment or situation changes.
