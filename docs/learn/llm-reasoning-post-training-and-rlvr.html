LLM Reasoning, Post-Training, and RLVR

A model can produce a correct answer for the wrong reason. Post-training tries to make useful behavior more likely, while verification and search try to select better behavior at inference time.

Start with intuition

The mental model

Think of a base model as a proposal generator. A reward or verifier supplies a score, policy optimization changes which proposals are likely, and test-time search spends additional computation choosing among them.

Inside this area

Reasoning models and chain-of-thought; reward modeling and preference feedback; RL with verifiable rewards; diffusion language models; general LLM training and evaluation.

Common methods

GRPO and policy optimization; process or outcome rewards; test-time search and sampling; verifiers; preference learning; alternative decoding orders.

Technical core

A common research pipeline

  1. 1Generate one or more candidate traces
  2. 2Score outcomes or intermediate steps
  3. 3Update the policy or select a candidate
  4. 4Test whether gains survive new tasks and larger compute budgets
Outcome-weighted policy updatemaximize E[r(x, y)] while limiting how far π moves from π₀

The reward r favors useful outputs y for prompt x. A reference-policy constraint helps prevent a model from exploiting the reward in destructive ways.

Make it concrete

Worked example: verifiable arithmetic

  1. The model produces four solution traces.
  2. A checker gives reward 1 only when the final answer is correct.
  3. Training increases the probability of rewarded traces.
  4. The key limitation: final-answer reward does not tell us whether the written reasoning is faithful.
Pause and predict

Show a strong answer

Look for evidence tied to mathematics and code accuracy; pass@k; reward-model calibration; reasoning coverage; compute-performance tradeoffs. Then check whether the result survives this limitation: A high score on cheaply verifiable tasks may not transfer to open-ended reasoning, and broad LLM training papers can inflate the area boundary.

Do not overclaim

A high score on cheaply verifiable tasks may not transfer to open-ended reasoning, and broad LLM training papers can inflate the area boundary.

Evaluation

How progress is tested

Mathematics and code accuracy; pass@k; reward-model calibration; reasoning coverage; compute-performance tradeoffs.

Useful comparison

Traditional supervised fine-tuning imitates target answers. RL-style post-training optimizes a reward signal. Test-time search may improve answers without changing model weights.

Paper 1 · Core caseOutstanding Paper · oral

The Flexibility Trap: Rethinking the Value of Arbitrary Order in Diffusion Language Models

Problem
Arbitrary-order diffusion language models have more flexible decoding paths, but that flexibility may avoid uncertain tokens needed for exploration.
Method
Compare arbitrary-order and autoregressive reasoning coverage, then apply standard GRPO while retaining parallel diffusion decoding.
Evidence
Mathematics and coding evaluations, pass@k coverage, matched RL comparisons, and 89.1% reported GSM8K accuracy.
Main result
In the tested reasoning settings, simpler autoregressive RL elicits stronger reasoning than preserving arbitrary token order.
Important limit
The conclusion is task-dependent; arbitrary ordering can still help constraint-satisfaction or non-reasoning tasks.

Paper 2 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

How much can language models memorize?

Problem
Memorization measurements often mix up information about a particular dataset with knowledge that generalizes beyond it.
Approach
Separate unintended dataset memorization from generalization, then estimate model capacity after removing the generalization component.
Evidence
Hundreds of transformers from 500K to 1.5B parameters trained across increasing dataset sizes.
Takeaway
The study reports capacity scaling and a transition from filling capacity with memorization toward greater generalization.
Caution
Capacity estimates depend on the formal separation and experimental regime; they are not a universal measure of what a model understands.

Paper 3 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

Maximum Likelihood Reinforcement Learning

Problem
Expected-reward RL can provide almost no learning signal on binary tasks where a model rarely succeeds.
Approach
Derive a compute-indexed objective from pass@k likelihood that moves from ordinary RL toward maximum likelihood as sampling compute grows.
Evidence
Policy-gradient experiments across several correctness-based domains against standard RL and GRPO.
Takeaway
The reported method improves both single-attempt and multi-attempt success in the evaluated settings.
Caution
The derivation focuses on binary correctness; open-ended rewards and evaluator errors need separate study.
Metadata cues
RL / policy optimization; Diffusion / flow · language/llm
