Graphs, Geometry, and Representation Learning

When relationships and symmetry matter, treating every input as an unrelated flat vector throws away useful structure.

Start with intuition

The mental model

Represent objects as nodes and relations as edges. Repeated message passing lets each node combine local information while respecting permutation or geometric symmetries.

Inside this area

Graph neural networks; geometric representation learning; manifolds; equivariant networks; relational and structured latent representations.

Common methods

Message passing; permutation equivariance; invariant and equivariant layers; spectral graph methods; geometric priors; contrastive and self-supervised objectives.

Technical core

A common research pipeline

  1. 1Choose nodes, edges, and features
  2. 2Send and aggregate messages
  3. 3Update node or graph representations
  4. 4Test expressivity and generalization beyond training graph sizes
Message-passing updatehᵢ′ = update(hᵢ, aggregate({message(hᵢ, hⱼ, eᵢⱼ)}))

Each node updates its representation using messages from neighbors. The aggregation should not depend on arbitrary node ordering.

Make it concrete

Worked example: shortest-path learning

  1. Nodes represent locations and edges carry distances.
  2. Messages propagate current distance estimates.
  3. Repeated layers imitate relaxation steps in a shortest-path algorithm.
  4. Success on small graphs does not prove generalization to larger graphs or longer paths.
Pause and predict

Which symmetries or structural assumptions are encoded, and are they valid for the data?

Show a strong answer

Look for evidence tied to generalization across graph size; algorithm execution; molecular and physical validity; symmetry tests; expressivity and oversmoothing. Then check whether the result survives this limitation: Fitting small graph instances does not prove an architecture learned an algorithm that generalizes to arbitrary size.

Do not overclaim

Fitting small graph instances does not prove an architecture learned an algorithm that generalizes to arbitrary size.

Evaluation

How progress is tested

Generalization across graph size; algorithm execution; molecular and physical validity; symmetry tests; expressivity and oversmoothing.

Useful comparison

Sequence models rely on order. Graph models rely on relations. Geometric models additionally encode how outputs should transform under rotations or translations.

Paper lab

Read three cases with a purpose

The core paper receives a complete problem-to-limit walkthrough. The next two cases are curated to show important breadth within the area.

Paper 1 · Core caseOral

Which Algorithms Can Graph Neural Networks Learn?

Problem
Message-passing GNNs often imitate algorithms empirically, but it is unclear when finite training examples imply correct behavior on larger inputs.
Method
Develop necessary conditions for learning algorithms with MPNNs, prove generalization properties, establish impossibility results, and derive stronger variants.
Evidence
Theorems covering shortest paths, minimum spanning trees, dynamic programming, and 0–1 knapsack, plus limits for tasks standard MPNNs cannot compute.
Main result
The framework separates algorithms learnable from small instances from tasks requiring more expressive message-passing architectures.
Important limit
Formal learnability conditions may require idealized data, optimization, precision, or architecture assumptions not guaranteed in practice.

Paper 2 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

Foundations of Equivariant Deep Learning: Unifying Graph and Sheaf Neural Networks

Why selected: Equivariant graph theory broadens the core case and shows another important part of this area.

Problem
Graph message passing and sheaf neural networks lack one common theory describing which transformations their layers should respect.
Approach
Use a face-poset view to define order-equivariant networks, characterize their linear maps, and build general equivariant layers.
Evidence
Formal characterizations and universal-approximation results, illustrated through graph and sheaf architectures.
Takeaway
The framework unifies familiar graph equivariance with a broader theory that also covers sheaf neural networks.
Caution
Expressivity and universal approximation do not guarantee learnability, data efficiency, or successful optimization in practice.

Paper 3 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

Necessary Conditions for Compositional Generalization of Embedding Models

Why selected: Compositional representation geometry broadens the core case and shows another important part of this area.

Problem
Training data covers only a small fraction of possible concept combinations, so representations must support combinations never seen together.
Approach
Formalize compositional requirements and derive additive factorization, near-orthogonality, and dimension constraints for embedding spaces.
Evidence
Theoretical results plus diagnostics on CLIP and SigLIP embeddings, related to their measured compositional generalization.
Takeaway
Factorized and nearly orthogonal concepts emerge as necessary structural conditions under the studied embedding setting.
Caution
Necessary conditions are not sufficient; empirical correlation and a linear-head analysis do not prove the cause of generalization.
