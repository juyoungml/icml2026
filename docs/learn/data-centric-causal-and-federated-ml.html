Data-Centric, Causal, and Federated ML

Better learning can come from changing the data, asking causal questions, or coordinating across separate data owners—not only from changing the model.

Start with intuition

The mental model

First identify what information is observed, where it lives, and what question is being asked. Prediction, intervention, continual adaptation, and federated coordination require different assumptions.

Inside this area

Data quality and labels; continual learning; causal discovery and inference; federated learning; distributed and heterogeneous clients.

Common methods

Replay and rehearsal; data valuation and selection; causal graphs and interventions; federated aggregation; debiasing; task adaptation.

Technical core

A common research pipeline

  1. 1Audit data quality and collection
  2. 2Choose prediction, causal, continual, or federated objective
  3. 3Control leakage, forgetting, and client differences
  4. 4Evaluate under realistic shifts and constraints
Prediction versus interventionP(Y | X=x) is not generally P(Y | do(X=x))

Observing X and actively changing X are different. Confounding can make predictive associations misleading for interventions.

Make it concrete

Worked example: continual robot learning

  1. A policy learns task A, then task B.
  2. Fine-tuning on B may overwrite behavior for A.
  3. A small replay buffer mixes prior examples into training.
  4. Measure both new-task learning and old-task forgetting across realistic task sequences.
Pause and predict

Does the method improve data selection or merely add a new scoring heuristic?

Show a strong answer

Look for evidence tied to forgetting and forward transfer; intervention accuracy; client heterogeneity; privacy and communication cost; label efficiency and robustness. Then check whether the result survives this limitation: Predictive gains do not establish causality, and simplified client or task sequences may hide real deployment constraints.

Do not overclaim

Predictive gains do not establish causality, and simplified client or task sequences may hide real deployment constraints.

Evaluation

How progress is tested

Forgetting and forward transfer; intervention accuracy; client heterogeneity; privacy and communication cost; label efficiency and robustness.

Useful comparison

Data-centric work improves the training evidence. Causal work targets interventions. Continual learning manages change over time. Federated learning coordinates data that cannot be centralized.

Paper lab

Read three cases with a purpose

The core paper receives a complete problem-to-limit walkthrough. The next two cases are curated to show important breadth within the area.

Paper 1 · Core caseOral

Pretrained Vision-Language-Action Models are Surprisingly Resistant to Forgetting in Continual Learning

Problem
Robot policies must learn new skills without destroying old ones, but continual-learning knowledge mostly comes from smaller models trained from scratch.
Method
Compare large pretrained VLA models with smaller behavior-cloning policies under sequential tasks and simple experience replay.
Evidence
Forgetting, forward learning, replay-buffer size, retained knowledge, and recovery after fine-tuning across robot-policy tasks.
Main result
Pretraining changes continual-learning dynamics: small replay buffers can produce little or no forgetting while retaining fast adaptation.
Important limit
The result may depend on the pretrained model, task similarity, replay access, and chosen robotics sequence; taxonomy placement also crosses into robotics.

Paper 2 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

A Recursive Decomposition Framework for Causal Structure Learning in the Presence of Latent Variables

Why selected: Causal discovery with hidden variables broadens the core case and shows another important part of this area.

Problem
Conditional-independence causal discovery becomes costly in high dimensions, and many divide-and-conquer methods assume that no hidden variables exist.
Approach
Recursively decompose the variable set, solve smaller latent-variable discovery problems, and reconstruct a global causal structure.
Evidence
Soundness and completeness results, synthetic efficiency tests, and an illustration on real-world data.
Takeaway
The framework extends divide-and-conquer causal discovery to settings that include latent variables.
Caution
The conclusion depends on structural assumptions and conditional-independence tests; a recovered graph does not establish every causal claim.
Metadata cues
Causal / data-centric · theory/synthetic

Paper 3 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

Incentivizing Truthfulness and Collaborative Fairness in Bayesian Learning

Why selected: Collaborative and federated incentives broadens the core case and shows another important part of this area.

Problem
Data contributors may manipulate what they submit to inflate rewards or reduce the value assigned to other contributors.
Approach
Combine contribution-based fairness with a hidden validation set and a mechanism designed to make truthful reporting an equilibrium.
Evidence
Equilibrium analysis supported by experiments on synthetic and real datasets.
Takeaway
The mechanism targets both fair contribution valuation and incentives for honest data sharing in collaborative learning.
Caution
Its guarantees rely on assumptions about the mediator, validation data, budget, and rational contributors; they do not automatically provide privacy.
Metadata cues
RL / policy optimization; Reasoning / test-time compute; Bayesian / probabilistic · theory/synthetic
