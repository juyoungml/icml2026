Systems and Efficient Foundation Models

A method is not efficient merely because one kernel runs faster. The entire workload must deliver acceptable quality at lower time, memory, energy, or cost.

Start with intuition

The mental model

Trace the full path from model weights and hardware through training or serving to user-visible latency and output quality. Bottlenecks move when batch size, sequence length, or hardware changes.

Inside this area

Large-scale training; optimizers and architectures; model compression; quantization; KV-cache and long context; inference and serving.

Common methods

Parallel optimization; spectral constraints; low precision; sparsity and MoE; cache pruning; kernels; speculative decoding; systems co-design.

Technical core

A common research pipeline

  1. 1Choose the real workload and hardware
  2. 2Measure a trustworthy baseline
  3. 3Change precision, architecture, cache, kernel, or schedule
  4. 4Report end-to-end cost together with quality regressions
End-to-end throughputuseful outputs / total wall-clock time

A local speedup matters only if it improves the full pipeline without unacceptable capability loss.

Make it concrete

Worked example: KV-cache reduction

  1. Autoregressive generation stores key and value states for earlier tokens.
  2. Longer contexts increase cache memory.
  3. Pruning or quantizing the cache reduces memory traffic.
  4. Measure latency and memory across sequence lengths, then check whether answer quality falls.
Pause and predict

Are results measured at realistic batch sizes, sequence lengths, hardware, and latency budgets?

Show a strong answer

Look for evidence tied to end-to-end throughput and latency; memory; training stability; energy or cost; quality regression; results across hardware, batch sizes, and sequence lengths. Then check whether the result survives this limitation: An isolated kernel speedup may disappear end to end, and efficiency can trade away reasoning, calibration, or safety.

Do not overclaim

An isolated kernel speedup may disappear end to end, and efficiency can trade away reasoning, calibration, or safety.

Evaluation

How progress is tested

End-to-end throughput and latency; memory; training stability; energy or cost; quality regression; results across hardware, batch sizes, and sequence lengths.

Useful comparison

Optimization research may improve the update rule. Systems research asks whether the implementation produces reliable end-to-end gains on real hardware.

Paper 1 · Core caseOral

Controlled LLM Training on Spectral Sphere

Problem
Large-model optimizers can control update scale while weights and activations still drift, weakening width-invariant stability guarantees.
Method
The Spectral Sphere Optimizer constrains module weights and updates, derives steepest descent on the spectral sphere, and implements it in Megatron.
Evidence
Pretraining Dense 1.7B, MoE 8B-A1B, and 200-layer DeepNet models against AdamW and Muon; activation, outlier, and router-balance analysis.
Main result
SSO reports faster or better optimization with bounded activations, suppressed outliers, and improved MoE load balance.
Important limit
The claim needs matched compute and hardware accounting, broader model families, and end-to-end cost analysis beyond optimizer stability.

Paper 2 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

POET-X: Memory-efficient LLM Training by Scaling Orthogonal Transformation

Problem
Spectrum-preserving training can improve stability but incur too much matrix-computation and memory overhead at large scale.
Approach
Rework orthogonal-equivalence transformations into a more scalable optimizer implementation.
Evidence
Billion-parameter pretraining, throughput and memory measurements, and comparison with optimizers that exceed a single H100’s memory.
Takeaway
The reported implementation retains stability benefits while making the approach practical at larger scale.
Caution
A single-hardware demonstration should be followed by matched compute, quality, and multi-platform evaluation.

Paper 3 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

FlashSinkhorn: IO-Aware Entropic Optimal Transport on GPU

Problem
GPU Sinkhorn solvers repeatedly move large interaction matrices through high-bandwidth memory.
Approach
Rewrite updates as attention-like reductions and fuse tiled Triton kernels so data can be streamed through on-chip memory.
Evidence
Point-cloud optimal-transport workloads on A100 GPUs and downstream optimization comparisons.
Takeaway
IO-aware reformulation reports large forward and end-to-end speedups with linear-memory operation.
Caution
The largest kernel speedup is not automatically the gain for every transport cost, hardware platform, or complete application.
