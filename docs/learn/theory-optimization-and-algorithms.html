Theory, Optimization, and Algorithms

Theory asks not only whether a method works, but under which assumptions it must work, may work, or cannot work.

Start with intuition

The mental model

Start with a mathematical setting, define the objective and assumptions, derive a guarantee or impossibility result, then test whether the predicted behavior appears outside the idealized model.

Inside this area

Statistical learning theory; online learning and bandits; convex and nonconvex optimization; Bayesian methods; transformer theory; numerical methods.

Common methods

Generalization bounds; regret analysis; stochastic optimization; spectral analysis; probabilistic inference; expressivity and impossibility results.

Technical core

A common research pipeline

  1. 1State the objects, assumptions, and quantity of interest
  2. 2Prove a bound, convergence rate, or impossibility
  3. 3Identify how the result scales
  4. 4Check which real settings violate the assumptions
Generalization gaptest error − training error

A small training error alone is not enough. Theory often studies when performance on observed samples transfers to unseen data.

Make it concrete

Worked example: delayed generalization

  1. A model quickly drives training error near zero.
  2. Test error remains high for many optimization steps.
  3. Weight decay gradually changes which solution is selected.
  4. A theorem predicts when test error should fall, but its linear assumptions must be checked before applying it to transformers.
Pause and predict

Which assumptions are doing the real work?

Show a strong answer

Look for evidence tied to proof assumptions and rates; synthetic experiments; sensitivity to dimension, data, compute, and hyperparameters; empirical checks of predicted behavior. Then check whether the result survives this limitation: A theorem can be correct yet rely on assumptions or scales that do not describe current production models.

Do not overclaim

A theorem can be correct yet rely on assumptions or scales that do not describe current production models.

Evaluation

How progress is tested

Proof assumptions and rates; synthetic experiments; sensitivity to dimension, data, compute, and hyperparameters; empirical checks of predicted behavior.

Useful comparison

Empirical work estimates what happens in measured settings. Theory explains consequences of stated assumptions. Strong research often uses each to challenge the other.

Paper 1 · Core caseOutstanding Paper Honorable Mention · oral

To Grok Grokking: Provable Grokking in Ridge Regression

Problem
Why can a model fit its training data early but generalize only much later?
Method
Analyze over-parameterized ridge regression trained by gradient descent with weight decay and derive quantitative grokking-time bounds.
Evidence
End-to-end proofs of overfitting, delayed generalization, and eventual low error; hyperparameter studies; nonlinear neural-network checks.
Main result
Grokking delay changes predictably with training conditions and can be amplified or removed through hyperparameter choices.
Important limit
The rigorous result is for classical linear regression; nonlinear experiments support transfer but do not prove the same mechanism for large models.

Paper 2 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

Equivalence of Context and Parameter Updates in Modern Transformer Blocks

Problem
Earlier context-as-weight-update theory covers simple transformers but not the gated, normalized, or expert-based blocks used today.
Approach
Construct exact implicit weight patches and characterize when inner and outer functions permit context effects to be represented as parameter changes.
Evidence
Analytical proofs for Gemma-style blocks followed by a constructive extension to multilayer and varied transformer designs.
Takeaway
The framework offers a common mathematical view of how prompts induce token-dependent effective computation.
Caution
Exact representational equivalence does not show that models learn, store, or use context in the same way during real training.

Transformer / attention · language/llm; theory/synthetic

Paper 3 · Abstract-based previewComparison case

Non-Euclidean Gradient Descent Operates at the Edge of Stability

Problem
Edge-of-stability behavior is common in deep learning, while standard theory is centered on Euclidean gradient descent.
Approach
Define geometry-aware directional smoothness and generalized sharpness for optimizers operating under other norms.
Evidence
Analysis plus neural-network experiments covering infinity-norm descent, coordinate methods, spectral descent, and Muon-like updates.
Takeaway
Progressive sharpening and threshold oscillation appear to extend beyond ordinary gradient descent.
Caution
A shared spectral description does not imply identical optimization dynamics or downstream behavior across optimizers.
Metadata cues
Graphs / geometry · theory/synthetic
